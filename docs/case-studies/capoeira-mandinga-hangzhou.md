## IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER

This case study focuses on **Capoeira Mandinga Hangzhou**, one branch of the international Capoeira Mandinga network founded by Mestre Marcelo Pereira. The research documents:
1. The founding and lineage of the parent organization (Capoeira Mandinga Oakland, 1984)
2. The expansion to China (first in Shanghai 2005, then Hangzhou 2014)
3. The unique pattern of diaspora practitioners founding schools in Asia
4. The challenges of maintaining schools across continents amid personal crises

**Not representative**: This case study focuses specifically on the Hangzhou branch and Chinese expansion, not the entire global Capoeira Mandinga network.

**Preliminary research**: Community verification welcome for current operational status and additional details.

**Current status uncertain**: The founding instructor (Alex Maltsev) relocated from China to Thailand due to personal circumstances. Current operational status of Hangzhou school requires verification.

## Group Evolution & Organizational History

### Founding: Oakland 1984

**Original Name**: Associação de Capoeira Senzala Grande (1984-1995)

**Name Change**: Capoeira Mandinga (1995)

**Why the Name Change?**

**Critical CDO Tradition in 1980s**: When a student graduated from Cordão de Ouro (CDO) in the 1980s, they were **not allowed to use the name of their mestre's group**. This was a mandatory tradition that only started to change by the early 1990s.

**Mestre Marcelo's Graduating Class**: All 15 graduated students of Mestre Suassuna from Mestre Marcelo's turma (class) had to come up with their own school names.

**Result**: This tradition led to the proliferation of separate capoeira groups in the 1980s-1990s, all with clear CDO lineage but distinct branding:
- Capoeira Mandinga (Mestre Marcelo)
- 14 other schools from same graduating class
- This pattern repeated across multiple CDO graduating classes

**"Mandinga" Meaning**:
- Portuguese: Sorcery, magic, trickery, charm
- Afro-Brazilian context: Refers to the deceptive, clever nature of capoeira
- Cultural significance: Captures the malícia (trickery/cunning) central to capoeira philosophy

### Legal & Organizational Structure

**Non-Profit Organization**: **The Capoeira Institute**
- Provides capoeira classes for children and adults
- Manages instructors and locations
- Organizes events and graduations

**Performing Arts Group**: **Brazil Dance Revue**
- Cultural performances
- Demonstrations
- Cultural exchange programs

## Organizational Model: Decentralized Network with Local Autonomy

### Classification

**Primary Model**: **Blessed Split / Affiliated Autonomous Network**

This is a unique hybrid model combining elements of:
1. **Blessed Split** (from CDO tradition requiring separate names)
2. **Autonomous Continuation** (local leaders have high autonomy)
3. **Network Affiliation** (shared identity, regular gatherings, but independent operations)

### Core Philosophy

**Origin from CDO Tradition**: The organizational model is rooted in the 1980s CDO tradition where graduated students were required to create their own schools with separate names. This forced decentralization from the beginning.

**Result**: Capoeira Mandinga operates as a loose network rather than a hierarchical organization.

### Organizational Structure

```
Mestre Marcelo Caveirinha (Oakland HQ)
  ├─ Regional Coordinators (semi-independent)
  │   ├─ Mestra Luar do Sertão (Tucson)
  │   ├─ Diego Pappalardo (Shanghai, China)
  │   ├─ Instrutor Alex (Hangzhou, supervises Hefei, Chengdu, Wenzhou) [relocated]
  │   └─ Other regional leaders (Italy, Iceland, etc.)
  └─ Local Instructors (autonomous within network)
      └─ Students
```

### Characteristics

**1. Local Autonomy**:
- Each location operates independently
- Local instructors make own decisions about:
  - Class schedules
  - Venue selection
  - Fee structures (often non-profit or donation-based)
  - Local marketing and recruitment
  - Social media management

**2. Shared Identity**:
- All use "Capoeira Mandinga" name
- Connection to Mestre Marcelo
- Participation in network events
- Shared teaching methodology (with local adaptations)

**3. No Central Control**:
- **No trademark licensing fees** (unlike CDO's Russian model)
- **No mandatory dues to headquarters** (unlike centralized franchises)
- **No approval required** for new locations (within network)
- **No central registry** of all instructors (unlike Muzenza)

**4. Network Activities**:
- **Annual Batizado**: Belt graduation ceremony, often conducted by Mestre Marcelo when visiting
- **Workshops and Seminars**: Regional and international
- **Camps**: Annual camps in Brazil (Ubatuba)
- **Mandinga Coast to Coast**: Online events connecting all locations globally
- **The Capoeira Institute**: Non-profit coordinating events and support

**5. Expansion Pattern**:
- **Student-initiated**: Students move to new cities and establish locations
- **Regional supervision**: Senior instructors may supervise clusters of locations (like Alex supervising 3+ Chinese cities)
- **Blessing from Mestre Marcelo**: New locations receive recognition during Batizado or visits
- **Organic growth**: No strategic expansion planning, grows through student migration

### Legal Structure

**United States**:
- **Non-profit**: The Capoeira Institute (501(c)(3) likely, not confirmed)
- **Performing Arts**: Brazil Dance Revue

**Other Countries**:
- Varies by location
- Often informal collectives (like Hangzhou: "non-profit collective")
- Some may be registered associations or informal groups

### Comparison to Other Models

| Aspect | Capoeira Mandinga | CDO (Russia) | Grupo Capoeira Brasil | Ngoma |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|----------------------|-------|
| **Origin** | Blessed split from CDO | Direct CDO branch | Original founding | Original founding |
| **Autonomy** | High - local control | Medium - 30+ halls, central coordination | Low - hierarchical arms | Very High - complete autonomy |
| **Fees to HQ** | None | Likely yes (for CDO branches) | Yes, structured | None |
| **Name Usage** | Shared "Mandinga" brand | Licensed "CDO" trademark | Shared "CB" identity | Voluntary use of "Ngoma" |
| **Approval Needed** | No (within network) | Yes (for new locations) | Yes (hierarchical) | No |
| **Expansion** | Student migration | Strategic/organizational | Organizational with approval | Student autonomous |

## Cultural Practices & Philosophy

### Style Classification

**Primary Style**: **Capoeira Contemporânea** (Contemporary Capoeira)

**Lineage Influence**:
- **Cordão de Ouro (CDO) tradition** through Mestre Suassuna → Mestre Marcelo
- CDO is known for contemporary blend (not strict Angola or Regional)
- Emphasis on musicality, acrobatics, and game flow

**Style Characteristics**:
- **Not strictly Angola**: Faster pace than traditional Angola
- **Not strictly Regional**: More emphasis on music and culture than Bimba's Regional
- **Contemporary Blend**: Combines elements of both styles with modern innovations

### Philosophy & Values

**Mission** (from Hangzhou website):
> "Celebrate and share the art of capoeira by enjoying the game and spreading knowledge of its rich Afro-Brazilian traditions."

**Core Values**:

**1. Inclusivity**:
- Welcomes beginners and people of all fitness levels
- All ages welcome
- All backgrounds welcome
- No prerequisites or prior martial arts experience needed

**2. Cultural Transmission**:
- **Not just physical**: Emphasis that capoeira involves music and culture (Diego's words)
- **Language**: Students learn Portuguese through songs
- **Instruments**: Training includes berimbau, pandeiro, atabaque, agogô
- **History**: Teaching Afro-Brazilian history and capoeira origins
- **Cultural context**: Connection to Brazilian identity and African roots

**3. Community Building**:
- **Cross-cultural friendships**: Chinese, expatriates, Brazilians training together
- **Social element**: "It's a good way to make new friends" (student testimonial)
- **Non-profit collective**: Emphasis on community rather than commercialization (Hangzhou)

**4. Holistic Development**:
- **Physical**: Fitness, flexibility, strength, coordination
- **Intellectual**: Strategy, rhythm, musical training
- **Emotional**: Self-expression, confidence, cultural identity
- **Social**: Communication, respect, community

### Training Methodology

**Class Structure** (typical):
1. **Warm-up**: Stretching, basic movements
2. **Technique**: Kicks, escapes, acrobatics
3. **Music**: Singing, instrument practice
4. **Jogo** (game): Practicing movements in the roda (circle)

**Belt System** (Hangzhou-specific):
- **Four main belts**: Green, Yellow, Blue, White
- **Intermediate levels**: Between main belts
- **Progression**: 2-3 training sessions weekly for ~1 year per level
- **Graduation**: Annual Batizado ceremony with Mestre Marcelo (when possible)

**Music Emphasis**:
- **Instruments taught**: Berimbau (primary), pandeiro, atabaque, agogô
- **Songs**: Traditional and modern capoeira songs
- **Portuguese language**: Learned through music
- **Cultural significance**: Understanding meaning and history of songs

### Unique Practices

**1. International Teacher Visits**:
- Mestre Marcelo visits China branches for Batizado and workshops
- Brings authentic connection to Brazilian roots
- Students experience direct transmission from founder

**2. Cultural Exchange**:
- **2018 Documentary**: Co-produced by Brazilian embassy, featured meeting of Brazilian capoeira mestres with Chinese martial arts masters in Beijing and Hangzhou
- Cross-cultural dialogue between Brazilian and Chinese martial arts
- Demonstration of capoeira's role as cultural ambassador

**3. Digital/Online Expansion** (Alex's Innovation):
- Online capoeira course reaching thousands in China
- Digital content in Russian and Chinese languages
- Use of technology to overcome geographic barriers

**4. Non-Profit Model**:
- Hangzhou operates as "non-profit collective"
- Emphasis on accessibility over profit
- Community-driven rather than commercial

### Challenges Addressed

**Language Barrier**:
- Chinese students learning Portuguese through songs
- English as bridge language in classes
- Translation of terminology and concepts

**Cultural Distance**:
- Explaining Afro-Brazilian history to Chinese students with no background
- Making Brazilian culture accessible and meaningful
- Balancing authenticity with local adaptation

**Small Community**:
- Less than 200 capoeiristas in China (1.4 billion people)
- Building critical mass for events and rodas
- Sustaining interest and retention

## Key Takeaway

**Capoeira Mandinga Hangzhou represents a modern pattern of capoeira globalization: non-Brazilian instructors founding schools in non-traditional capoeira countries through decentralized networks.**

### For Database Design:

1. **Blessed Split Tracking**: Schema must distinguish between:
   - Organizational affiliation (Capoeira Mandinga)
   - Lineage chain (CDO → Mestre Suassuna → Mestre Marcelo)
   - These are NOT the same - need separate fields

2. **Multi-Organizational Certification**:
   - Instructors may be certified by multiple organizations
   - Primary affiliation ≠ only certification source
   - Need `certifications` table separate from `primary_organization`

3. **Diaspora Instructor Pattern**:
   - Instructor nationality ≠ teaching location ≠ student nationality
   - Track: instructor_nationality, teaching_country, student_demographics separately
   - Important for understanding modern global capoeira transmission

4. **Ambiguous Operational Status**:
   - Not all schools are cleanly "open" or "closed"
   - Need statuses: active | inactive | relocated | uncertain | defunct
   - Need `last_verified_date` and `verification_needed` flags

5. **Digital/Online Expansion**:
   - Track both physical locations and online presence
   - Metrics: physical_students, online_followers, geographic_reach
   - Location type: physical | online | hybrid

### For Genealogy Tree Visualization:

1. **Show Organizational vs. Lineage Splits**:
   - Visual distinction between blessed splits (Mandinga from CDO) and lineage continuity
   - Mestre Marcelo is "split from CDO" organizationally but "CDO lineage" culturally
   - Tree needs to show both dimensions

2. **Cross-Organizational Connections**:
   - Alex certified by CDO mestres while being Mandinga member
   - Need visual indicators of hidden cross-organizational relationships
   - These connections are important for understanding network effects

3. **Geographic Layers**:
   - Ability to filter/view by continent, country, city
   - Asia expansion pattern different from Europe or South America
   - "First in country" pioneers should be visually distinct

4. **Temporal Uncertainty**:
   - Visual indication when current status is uncertain
   - Different from "closed" - could be active, could be inactive
   - Invite community to update/verify

1. **Digital Natives Easier to Research**:

   - Modern instructors with digital footprints yield more complete case studies

### Cultural Insight:

**The Mandinga China story reveals the multi-layered nature of modern capoeira transmission**:

1. **Brazil** (origin) → **USA** (diaspora 1984) → **Italy/Russia** (second diaspora 2000s) → **China** (third diaspora 2005+) → **Chinese students** (first indigenous generation)

2. Each layer adds:
   - Cultural translation challenge
   - Language barrier (Portuguese → English → Chinese)
   - Geographic distance from Brazilian roots
   - Questions about authenticity and legitimacy

3. **Yet cultural transmission succeeds because**:
   - Clear lineage documentation (Suassuna → Marcelo → Diego → Alex)
   - Regular visits from senior teachers (Mestre Marcelo to China)
   - Emphasis on music, culture, not just physical technique
   - Strong network identity (Mandinga community spans continents)
   - Digital tools overcome geographic barriers (online courses, Coast to Coast events)

4. **The "pioneer" mindset matters**:
   - Alex self-describes as "pioneer of capoeira in China"
   - Pioneers face unique challenges (tiny community, high cultural barriers)
   - But pioneers also create lasting impact ("incredible students now leading groups")
   - Empowering next generation becomes mission after personal setback

At what point does capoeira transmitted through multiple diaspora layers (Brazil → USA → Italy → China → Chinese students) diverge too far from "authentic" capoeira? Or does the focus on lineage, music, culture, and regular connection to senior teachers maintain authenticity regardless of geographic/cultural distance?

The Capoeira Mandinga Hangzhou story suggests that with:
