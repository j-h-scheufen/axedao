## IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER

This case study documents a **historically and culturally profound moment**: the founding of the first Capoeira Angola school in the country of Angola, bringing an Afro-Brazilian art form with African roots back to its namesake homeland.

**This represents**:
1. Cultural reclamation - reconnecting with pre-slavery African heritage
2. Reverse diaspora - Brazil → Africa (reversing historical slave trade flow)
3. First school in namesake country - capoeira "Angola" returns to Angola
4. Post-conflict nation-building through cultural identity

**Not representative**: This case study focuses on one school in one city, not the entire capoeira landscape in Angola or Africa.

**Preliminary research**: Based on 2021 reporting and limited online presence. Current operational status and growth require community verification.

**Historical Context**: Angola experienced a devastating 27-year civil war (1975-2002) with 500k-800k deaths and over 1 million displaced. The founder went into exile at age 15 and returned in 2014 to help rebuild his country through capoeira.

## Complete Lineage Chain: From Pastinha to Angola

This represents one of the most historically significant lineage chains in capoeira - from Mestre Pastinha (who formalized Capoeira Angola in Brazil) back to the country of Angola itself.

### The Lineage

```
Generation 1: Mestre Pastinha (1889-1981)
  ├─ Founded modern Capoeira Angola in Salvador, Bahia
  ├─ Opened Centro Esportivo de Capoeira Angola (1941)
  ├─ Traveled to Senegal for First World Festival of Black Arts (1966)
  └─ Said before death: "Capoeira Angola is here in Brazil and will return to Angola again"

Generation 2: João Grande & João Pequeno (Principal students)
  ├─ Ran Pastinha's academy when Pastinha went blind
  ├─ Preserved Pastinha's teachings
  └─ Taught next generation in 1950s-1960s

Generation 3: Mestre Moraes (Pedro Moraes Trindade, born 1950)
  ├─ Started training age 7-10 at Pastinha's academy (1957-1960)
  ├─ Trained under João Grande & João Pequeno
  ├─ States João Grande made strongest impression on him
  ├─ Founded GCAP (Grupo de Capoeira Angola Pelourinho) - 1980
  ├─ Returned to Salvador 1982, revitalized Capoeira Angola
  ├─ Convinced João Grande to come out of retirement
  └─ Known as "the resurrectionor of the tradition"

Generation 4: Contramestre Kimbundo (Lucio Ngungi, born ~1985)
  ├─ Trained in Regional first (7 years)
  ├─ Specifically sought out Mestre Moraes for Angola training
  ├─ Earned Contramestre rank
  ├─ Returned to Angola 2014
  └─ Founded Escola de Capoeira Angola Okupandula

Generation 5: Students in Luanda, Angola (current generation)
  ├─ Kelly (17 years old as of 2021)
  ├─ Marcos (18, training 5 years, so started ~2016)
  └─ Others (small but keen cohort)
```

### Lineage Significance

**Generational Distance**: 4 generations from Mestre Pastinha

**Geographic Arc**:
1. **Pastinha** (Salvador, Bahia, Brazil) - Formalized Capoeira Angola
2. **João Grande/Pequeno** (Salvador, Brazil) - Preserved tradition
3. **Mestre Moraes** (Salvador → various) - Resurrected & spread Angola tradition
4. **Contramestre Kimbundo** (Exile → Luanda, Angola) - **Brought it "home" to Angola**

**The Prophecy Fulfilled**: Mestre Pastinha's words before he died (1981):
> "Capoeira Angola is here in Brazil and will return to Angola again. Capoeira de Angola is the seed and can never die."

Contramestre Kimbundo, trained in Pastinha's lineage, literally fulfilled this prophecy by opening the first Capoeira Angola school in Angola in 2014, 33 years after Pastinha's death.

## Cultural Significance: "Capoeira Angola" Returns to Angola

This is not just another capoeira school opening in a new country. This represents a profound cultural moment.

### The Name Connection

**Capoeira Angola**: The name itself refers to the African origins. "Angola" in capoeira context means:
- The traditional, ritualistic style (vs. Regional, which is modernized)
- Connection to African slaves from Angola/Congo region brought to Brazil
- Preservation of African movement aesthetics (low to ground, strategic)
- Slower, more deliberate game with emphasis on tradition

**Country of Angola**: The actual nation, located in southwest Africa

**Historical Question**: Did capoeira actually originate in what is now Angola?
- **Speculative**: Slaves from Luanda docks came from across southern and central Africa, not just Angola
- **Possible connection**: N'golo/engolo (zebra dance) practiced by Nyaneka and Nkhumbi peoples of southwest Angola
- **Likely reality**: Capoeira developed in Brazil as a synthesis of multiple African traditions, with Angolan traditions being one major influence

**Regardless of historical accuracy**: The symbolic significance of bringing "Capoeira Angola" to Angola is profound.

### Mestre Pastinha's Prophecy (1966 & Before Death)

**1966 Senegal Trip**: Mestre Pastinha traveled to Dakar, Senegal for the First World Festival of Black Arts
- Brought João Grande, Gato Preto, Gildo Alfinete, Roberto Satanás, and Camafeu de Oxossi
- Demonstrated capoeira in Africa for first time
- Locals told them: "Capoeira is in Brasil, your capoeira angola is in Brasil. Our people took it there and now you are showing it here."

**Before Death (1981)**: Pastinha said:
> "Capoeira Angola is here in Brazil and will return to Angola again. Capoeira de Angola is the seed and can never die."

**Fulfilled 2014**: Contramestre Kimbundo, trained in Pastinha's lineage (via João Grande/Pequeno → Moraes), opened the first Angola school in Angola, 33 years after this prophecy.

### Reverse Diaspora Pattern

**Historical Flow**:
```
1500s-1800s: African slaves (Angola, Congo region) → Brazil
              ↓
            Capoeira develops in Brazil (synthesis of African + Brazilian influences)
              ↓
1941: Mestre Pastinha formalizes "Capoeira Angola" (honoring African roots)
              ↓
2014: Contramestre Kimbundo brings Capoeira Angola → Angola (reverse flow)
```

**This represents**:
- Cultural homecoming
- Post-colonial cultural reclamation
- Acknowledging African roots by literally returning to Africa
- Healing historical trauma (slavery) through cultural exchange

### What It Means for Angolans

**For Angolan students**:
- Connection to something that bears their country's name

**Duration**: 27 years
**Period**: From independence from Portugal (1975) to peace agreement (2002)

**Internally Displaced**: Over 1 million
**Refugees**: Thousands fled to neighboring countries

- UNITA (National Union for the Total Independence of Angola) - US/South Africa-backed
- FNLA (National Liberation Front of Angola)

**Impact**:
- Devastated infrastructure
- Millions of landmines (still impeding farming today)
- Lost generation (children grew up knowing only war)
- Massive refugee crisis

## The Engolo/N'golo Connection: Research Linking Capoeira to African Traditions

This section provides crucial context for why bringing Capoeira Angola to Angola is culturally significant beyond the name.

### What is Engolo/N'golo?

**Definition**: A combat game/ritual dance practiced by the Nyaneka and Nkhumbi peoples of southwest Angola

**Context**: Coming-of-age ritual where young men compete for marriage rights

**Movements**:
- Mimics fighting zebras
- Low kicks and sweeps
- Acrobatic movements
- Performed in a circle (like capoeira roda)

**Geographic Location**: Southwest Angola (far from Luanda where Okupandula is located)

### Historical Speculation: Engolo → Capoeira?

**Theory**:
- Enslaved people from Angola brought engolo traditions to Brazil
- Combined with other African martial traditions, indigenous practices, and Brazilian innovations
- Developed into capoeira over centuries

**Evidence**:
- Movement similarities (low kicks, sweeps, acrobatics)
- Circle formation (roda)
- Ritualistic elements
- Oral histories linking capoeira to Angola

**Caution**:
- Slaves from Luanda docks came from across southern/central Africa, not just Angola
- Capoeira likely synthesis of multiple African traditions + Brazilian development
- Direct engolo → capoeira link is speculative but plausible

### Mestre Cobra Mansa's Research (2006-2011)

**Researchers**:
- **Mestre Cobra Mansa** (FICA co-founder, PhD candidate at time)
- **Matthias Röhrig Assunção** (historian, University of Essex)
- **Richard Pakleppa** (filmmaker)
- **Christine Dettmann** (ethnomusicologist)

**Research Trips**:
- **2006**: First trip to Angola (University of Essex grant)
  - Visited Luanda (where Brazilian capoeira already established)
  - Traveled to Mucope village
  - Witnessed engolo performed (same village where painter Neves e Sousa sketched zebra dance 50 years earlier)

- **2010 & 2011**: Extended research (British Arts and Humanities Research Council funding)
  - Revisited initial sites
  - Extended to other villages and communities
  - Documented musical instruments, dances, combat games
  - Focus on Nyaneka and Nkhumbi peoples

**Documentary**: "Jogo de Corpo: Capoeira e Ancestralidade" (2013)

**Mestre Cobra Mansa's PhD**: "Gingando on the Kalunga line: Capoeira Angola, Engolo and building of ancestrality" (UFBA, 2019)

### Significance for Okupandula

**Cultural Validation**:
- Research provides evidence (not proof) of Angola-capoeira connection
- Strengthens claim that capoeira Angola has roots in Angola (country)
- Validates Ngungi's mission to "go to the roots of my country"

**Identity Building**:
- Angolan students can see concrete link between their country and capoeira
- Not just "something from Brazil" but "something with Angolan DNA"
- Pride in contributing to development of global art form

**Historical Narrative**:
- Pre-slavery African tradition (engolo)
  ↓
- Slavery disrupts but traditions survive in diaspora
  ↓
- Capoeira develops in Brazil (synthesis including Angolan traditions)
  ↓
- Capoeira "returns home" to Angola (Okupandula 2014)
  ↓
- Reconnection with pre-slavery heritage
