## IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER

This case study represents preliminary research on ONE Capoeira group (Capoeira Batuque / Brasil Brasil Cultural Center) for the purpose of understanding organizational structures, relationship patterns, and data modeling needs for a genealogy tree database.

**This is NOT**:
- A comprehensive history of capoeira in Japan or USA
- A representative sample of Angola tradition groups
- An endorsement or evaluation of this group vs. others
- A complete or authoritative account

**Limitations**:
- Research conducted via web search and publicly available sources only
- No interviews with group members or founder
- No verification from primary sources
- Limited information about exact founding year of Japan branch (batizados since 2009, but establishment date unclear)
- JavaScript-heavy website prevented full extraction of organizational details
- USA headquarters operations not extensively researched (focus on Japan branch)

This case study is part of ongoing research to identify patterns across diverse Capoeira organizations worldwide to inform database schema design.

## Key Takeaway

**Capoeira Batuque Japão represents a clear example of international branch representation in the capoeira diaspora**:

### 1. Clear Traditional Angola Lineage

**Mestre Waldemar → Mestre Amen → Japan instructors** provides transparent lineage chain to legendary Bahian Angola master. This contrasts with Capoeira Zoador's unknown lineage and demonstrates importance of lineage documentation for genealogy trees.

**Parallel Lineage Significance**: Mestre Waldemar's lineage is independent of (parallel to) Mestre Pastinha's Angola lineage, demonstrating that the genealogy tree must handle **multiple independent Angola traditions** rather than single Angola "trunk."

### 2. Film as Cultural Bridge

**"Only the Strong" (1993)** choreography by Mestre Amen represents use of Hollywood film industry to bring capoeira to mainstream American awareness, potentially facilitating:
- Financial sustainability for Brasil Brasil Cultural Center (LA)
- Increased interest in capoeira in USA in 1990s
- Cultural legitimacy for later international expansion

**Data Model Implication**: Track film/media appearances as they can significantly impact group growth and cultural awareness.

### 3. Branch Model with Distributed Leadership

**Four co-equal senior instructors** (2 Contra Mestres, 2 Professores) in Japan branch represents:
- **Resilience**: Not dependent on single instructor
- **Gender balance**: 50% female leadership in traditional Angola (historically male-dominated)
- **Collective leadership**: Multiple decision-makers
- **Founder visits**: Regular connection to Mestre Amen for batizados maintains lineage authority

**Organizational Pattern**: Semi-autonomous branch - day-to-day operations independent, but major events (graduations/batizados) conducted by founder.

### 4. Long-Term Development Model

**13+ years minimum to reach Professor Level 3** demonstrates:
- Traditional Angola emphasis on slow, deep learning
- Contrast with faster-paced Regional/Contemporânea progressions
- Quality over quantity expansion strategy

**Comparison**:
- **Batuque**: 13+ years to Professor Level 3 (depth focus)
- **Zoador**: Unknown progression time
- **Other groups**: Varies widely (some 8-10 years to Mestre, others 15-20+)

### 5. Geographic Spread Pattern

**Kanto region concentration (Tokyo, Yokohama, Chiba) + Kochi** suggests:
- Urban concentration (Kanto metropolitan area)
- One outlier (Kochi on Shikoku island)
- Potential visiting instructor model for distant locations
