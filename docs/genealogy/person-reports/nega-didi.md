## Biography / Biografia

### English

Nega Didi was one of the few women who practiced capoeira in Salvador during the 1930s and 1940s - an era when the art was deeply hostile to female participation. Sources describe her as "outra valentona, sem registros, provavelmente entre os anos 30 e 40" (another valentona, without records, probably between the 30s and 40s).

She was part of a small but determined group of women who reportedly "se fizeram passar por homens" (disguised themselves as men) to gain acceptance in the male-dominated rodas of capoeira. This group included Maria 12 Homens, Satanás, Calça Rala, and Maria Pára o Bonde - women who became known as the "grandes referências femininas" (great female references) of strength, courage, and determination from this era.

Her apelido "Nega Didi" follows the common Brazilian Portuguese diminutive naming pattern, where "Nega" is an affectionate term for a Black woman and "Didi" is likely a nickname or diminutive of a given name. Such names were common in the informal capoeira and street culture of Salvador.

According to sources, Nega Didi was among the students of Mestre Bimba, who opened Salvador's first formal capoeira academy. Bimba was notable for accepting women into his academy during a time when this was controversial. However, the historical record notes that female classes at Bimba's academy faced ongoing challenges: they reportedly never completed full courses due to social pressures including interference from jealous boyfriends, pregnancies, and romantic entanglements with male students.

Like other women of her era, Nega Didi "viveu sem registros" (lived without records). No civil name, specific dates, or detailed biographical information survive. Her story exists primarily in the realm of oral tradition and collective memory, preserved in the mentions by later researchers documenting the forgotten history of women in early capoeira.

These women - the valentonas of the 1930s-1940s - broke significant barriers in an art form that was both criminalized (until 1940) and explicitly coded as masculine. Their courage in entering and persisting in this hostile environment paved the way for future generations of women in capoeira.

## Origin of Apelido / Origem do Apelido

"Nega Didi" follows the common Brazilian Portuguese diminutive naming pattern:
- **Nega** - Affectionate term for a Black woman in Brazilian Portuguese
- **Didi** - Likely a diminutive or nickname of a given name (possibly Odília, Edilene, or similar)

Such nicknames were common in the informal capoeira and street culture of early 20th century Salvador.

### Context: Bimba's Female Classes

Mestre Bimba attempted to form at least three classes of female students:
- Earliest known: 1930s (three women, did not complete course)
- 1940s: Nega Didi and contemporaries
- Latest known: 1967/68 (four students including Rosa Rubra/Nalvinha, Bimba's daughter)

None of these female classes completed the full course due to social pressures.
