## Biography / Biografia

### English

Americano is known primarily through a brief mention in Madame Satã's prison memoirs. Although Madame Satã did not consider himself a capoeira practitioner, in his memoirs he mentions some "capoeira rogues" (malandros capoeiras) who were imprisoned in Dois Rios alongside him, including Sete Coroas (who was from Bahia), Edgar, and Americano.

The Dois Rios Correctional Colony (Colônia Correcional de Dois Rios) on Ilha Grande was established in 1894 to imprison "drunkards, beggars, prostitutes, capoeiras, and vagrants". The colony became notorious for its harsh conditions, earning it the nickname "Ilha da Maldição" (Isle of Damnation).

A potentially significant connection exists: Mestre Celso Carvalho do Nascimento (born 1939), a respected capoeira mestre from Rio de Janeiro, mentioned in an interview that he had a cousin nicknamed "Americano" who was imprisoned at the Colony and knew how to do tiririca. According to Mestre Celso, tiririca involved playing on the ground, throwing leg kicks at someone standing up to make them fall, and delivering headbutts. Practitioners at Ilha Grande avoided calling it capoeira.

Whether Mestre Celso's cousin is the same Americano mentioned by Madame Satã remains unverified. If they are the same person, it would place Americano's imprisonment in a timeframe that overlaps with both Madame Satã and Sete Coroas. If different, the coincidence of the same unusual nickname and the same prison suggests tiririca practitioners at Dois Rios may have formed an identifiable group.

Nothing else is known about Americano—not his full name, origin, the nature of his crimes, or his fate after imprisonment.

## Historical Context / Contexto Histórico

### English

Americano represents the generation of capoeiristas who were imprisoned under Brazil's 1890 Penal Code. The Dois Rios Correctional Colony was one of the primary destinations for those arrested under laws criminalizing capoeira and vagrancy.

**Tiririca:** According to Mestre Celso, tiririca was distinct from capoeira—it involved ground play, leg kicks from a lying position aimed at standing opponents, and headbutts. This fighting system may represent a variant of capoeira that developed or was preserved in prison environments. Seu Júlio, another long-term prisoner, confirmed that "capoeira and tiririca mean one thing only" but that practitioners at Dois Rios avoided calling it capoeira. This reflects both the continued stigmatization of the art and possible regional/contextual variations in terminology.

### Português

Americano representa a geração de capoeiristas que foram presos sob o Código Penal de 1890 do Brasil. A Colônia Correcional de Dois Rios foi um dos principais destinos para os presos sob leis que criminalizavam a capoeira e a vadiagem.

**Tiririca:** Segundo Mestre Celso, a tiririca era distinta da capoeira—envolvia jogo no chão, chutes das pernas a partir de uma posição deitada mirados em oponentes em pé, e cabeçadas. Este sistema de luta pode representar uma variante da capoeira que se desenvolveu ou foi preservada em ambientes prisionais. Seu Júlio, outro prisioneiro de longa data, confirmou que "capoeira e tiririca significam uma coisa só", mas que os praticantes em Dois Rios evitavam chamar de capoeira. Isso reflete tanto a contínua estigmatização da arte quanto possíveis variações regionais/contextuais na terminologia.
