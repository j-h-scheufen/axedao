## Biography / Biografia

### English

Maria Pára o Bonde (also spelled "Maria para o bonde") was one of the few women who practiced capoeira in Salvador during the 1930s and 1940s - an era when the art was deeply hostile to female participation.

Her distinctive apelido translates roughly to "Maria Stops the Streetcar" - a colorful nickname suggesting either an incident involving a streetcar or perhaps a metaphor for her ability to stop people in their tracks. Such vivid apelidos were common in the street culture of early 20th century Salvador, where nicknames often commemorated notable events, physical characteristics, or feats of strength and daring.

She was part of a small but determined group of women who reportedly "se fizeram passar por homens" (disguised themselves as men) to gain acceptance in the male-dominated rodas of capoeira. This group included Maria 12 Homens, Satanás, Calça Rala, and Nega Didi - women who became known as the "grandes referências femininas" (great female references) of strength, courage, and determination from this era.

These women became known as valentonas - brave, tough women who defied social norms to practice an art form that was both criminalized (until 1940) and explicitly coded as masculine.

According to sources, Maria Pára o Bonde was among the students of Mestre Bimba, who opened Salvador's first formal capoeira academy. Bimba was notable for accepting women into his academy during a time when this was controversial. However, the historical record notes that female classes at Bimba's academy faced ongoing challenges: they reportedly never completed full courses due to social pressures including interference from jealous boyfriends, pregnancies, and romantic entanglements with male students.

Like other women of her era, Maria Pára o Bonde lived without records. No civil name, specific dates, or detailed biographical information survive. Her story exists primarily in the realm of oral tradition and collective memory, preserved in the mentions by later researchers documenting the forgotten history of women in early capoeira.

## Origin of Apelido / Origem do Apelido

"Maria Pára o Bonde" (also "Maria para o bonde") translates to "Maria Stops the Streetcar". The nickname's origin is unknown but likely commemorates one of the following:

1. **Literal interpretation**: An actual incident where she stopped (or blocked) a streetcar
2. **Metaphorical meaning**: Her ability to "stop people in their tracks" - i.e., her formidable presence or fighting ability
3. **Street culture event**: Some memorable confrontation or feat involving a streetcar

Salvador had an extensive streetcar (bonde) network in the early 20th century, and bondes were common settings for street life, confrontations, and public encounters. Such colorful apelidos commemorating specific events were typical of the capoeira and malandragem culture of the era.
