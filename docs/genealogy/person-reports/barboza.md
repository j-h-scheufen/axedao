## Biography / Biografia

### English

Barboza was a capoeirista who practiced in Salvador, Bahia, during the 1930s. He was part of the legendary Sunday training gatherings at Matatu Preto—a lowland area in the Matatu neighborhood where capoeiristas would meet in an open square (terreiro) to train and play.

According to Mestre Canjiquinha's 1989 testimony about the Matatu Preto group: "On Sundays, all those capoeiristas would come—Onça Preta, Geraldo Chapeleiro, Totonho Maré, Creoni, Chico Três Pedaços, Pedro Paulo Barroquinha, the late Barboza, and this citizen called Antonio Raimundo, nicknamed by all Aberrê".

The reference to "finado Barboza" (the late Barboza) indicates he had already passed away by 1989 when Canjiquinha recorded this testimony. This is the only known documentation of Barboza—no records survive of his full name, his trade, his teachers, or the circumstances of his death.

Barboza trained alongside some of the most significant figures in capoeira history: Mestre Aberrê (who led the sessions and later trained both Canjiquinha and Caiçara), Onça Preta (who would go on to establish capoeira Angola in Rio de Janeiro), and Totonho de Maré (co-founder of Gengibirra and recognized as one of the finest capoeiristas of his era).

Like many capoeiristas of the pre-modern era, Barboza exists in history primarily as a name in an oral tradition—a figure remembered by his contemporaries but whose individual story was never recorded. His presence in Canjiquinha's testimony reminds us how much of capoeira's history was preserved only in the memories of those who lived it.

## Historical Context / Contexto Histórico

### English
The Matatu Preto training group represents an important chapter in Salvador capoeira history. Located in a lowland area of the Matatu neighborhood, this open-air terreiro hosted Sunday gatherings where capoeiristas from across Salvador came to train and play. Led by Aberrê, this group included several practitioners who would become foundational figures—Totonho de Maré and Geraldo Chapeleiro were co-founders of Gengibirra; Onça Preta would later bring capoeira Angola to Rio de Janeiro.

Barboza's documentation exists solely through Canjiquinha's recollection, reminding us that for every famous mestre, there were dozens of practitioners whose stories were never fully recorded.

### Português
O grupo de treino do Matatu Preto representa um capítulo importante na história da capoeira de Salvador. Localizado em uma baixada no bairro do Matatu, este terreiro ao ar livre abrigava encontros de domingo onde capoeiristas de toda Salvador vinham treinar e jogar. Liderado por Aberrê, este grupo incluía vários praticantes que se tornariam figuras fundamentais—Totonho de Maré e Geraldo Chapeleiro foram co-fundadores da Gengibirra; Onça Preta mais tarde levaria a capoeira Angola para o Rio de Janeiro.

A documentação de Barboza existe unicamente através da lembrança de Canjiquinha, nos lembrando que para cada mestre famoso, havia dezenas de praticantes cujas histórias nunca foram completamente registradas.
