## Biography / Biografia

### English

Boa Viagem is known only through the testimony of Seu Júlio (Julio de Almeida), a former prisoner of the Dois Rios Correctional Colony on Ilha Grande. In a 2012 interview when Seu Júlio was 82 years old, he recounted his experiences practicing capoeira in Rio de Janeiro before his imprisonment.

When Seu Júlio lived in the Catacumba slum, located on the hills surrounding the Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas, he practiced capoeira, running, and boxing with "nortistas" (Bahians) at the lagoon. Among his training partners was "a friend known as Boa Viagem," who also served time at Dois Rios.

The Catacumba favela existed from approximately the 1930s until its removal in 1970. At its peak, it housed around 15,000 residents, many of whom were migrants from the state of Maranhão who arrived in the 1940s. The community was located in a privileged position overlooking the lagoon, between Ipanema and Copacabana, but lacked basic infrastructure—residents had to share fifteen public water spouts near the asphalt.

The capoeira practiced at Catacumba and Rodrigo de Freitas was part of a broader pattern of informal training that occurred in Rio's favelas and public spaces during the mid-twentieth century. After the near-extinction of capoeira following the brutal Republican-era suppression, the art survived in "more discreet locations," including popular neighborhoods and favelas, where techniques were passed on by malandros and practitioners who had learned from Bahian migrants.

Boa Viagem's imprisonment at Dois Rios connects him to a larger community of capoeiristas who served time at the colony, including Sete Coroas (from Bahia), Edgar, and Americano—all mentioned in Madame Satã's prison memoirs. At Dois Rios, the fighting art was often called "tiririca" rather than capoeira. As Seu Júlio explained: "capoeira and tiririca mean one thing only, it depends a lot on the teacher and (...) the lightness, the instruction (...) that was different from capoeira."

Nothing else is known about Boa Viagem—not his full name, the nature of his crimes, or his fate after imprisonment. His apelido, meaning "good journey" or "bon voyage," is also the name of a traditional capoeira farewell song and a neighborhood in Salvador, making research challenging.

A capoeira praticada na Catacumba e na Rodrigo de Freitas fazia parte de um padrão mais amplo de treinamento informal que ocorria nas favelas e espaços públicos do Rio em meados do século XX. Após a quase extinção da capoeira seguindo a brutal repressão da era republicana, a arte sobreviveu em "locais mais discretos," incluindo bairros populares e favelas, onde as técnicas eram transmitidas por malandros e praticantes que haviam aprendido com migrantes baianos.

A prisão de Boa Viagem em Dois Rios o conecta a uma comunidade maior de capoeiristas que cumpriram pena na colônia, incluindo Sete Coroas (da Bahia), Edgar e Americano—todos mencionados nas memórias de prisão de Madame Satã. Em Dois Rios, a arte da luta era frequentemente chamada de "tiririca" em vez de capoeira. ## Historical Context / Contexto Histórico

### English

Boa Viagem represents a poorly documented but important period in Rio de Janeiro's capoeira history—the mid-twentieth century survival of the art in favelas and informal spaces. After the severe Republican-era crackdown that nearly eliminated organized capoeira in Rio (1889-1930s), the fighting techniques survived through informal transmission in popular neighborhoods.

The Catacumba favela where Boa Viagem lived was one of the major hillside communities surrounding Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas in Rio's wealthy South Zone. Despite its proximity to affluent neighborhoods like Ipanema and Copacabana, Catacumba lacked basic services. The favela was removed in 1970 as part of Rio's "defavelization" policy, and residents were relocated to distant housing projects in the suburbs.

The practice of training at Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas with "nortistas" (Bahians) reflects the arrival of Bahian capoeira styles in Rio during the 1950s. Bahian migrants brought Regional and Angola styles developed by Mestre Bimba and Mestre Pastinha, which merged with whatever local techniques had survived in Rio to form the basis of contemporary capoeira.

### Português

Após a severa repressão da era republicana que quase eliminou a capoeira organizada no Rio (1889-1930s), as técnicas de luta sobreviveram através da transmissão informal em bairros populares.

A favela da Catacumba onde Boa Viagem morava era uma das principais comunidades nos morros ao redor da Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas na rica Zona Sul do Rio. Apesar de sua proximidade com bairros abastados como Ipanema e Copacabana, a Catacumba carecia de serviços básicos. A favela foi removida em 1970 como parte da política de "desfavelização" do Rio, e os moradores foram relocados para conjuntos habitacionais distantes nos subúrbios.

A prática de treinar na Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas com "nortistas" (baianos) reflete a chegada dos estilos baianos de capoeira no Rio durante os anos 1950. Migrantes baianos trouxeram os estilos Regional e Angola desenvolvidos por Mestre Bimba e Mestre Pastinha, que se fundiram com quaisquer técnicas locais que haviam sobrevivido no Rio para formar a base da capoeira contemporânea.
