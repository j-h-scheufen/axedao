## Biography / Biografia

### English

Alfredo Martins Teixeira, known as Caboclinho, was a saveirista (operator of traditional saveiro sailing vessels) and capoeirista who became one of the most notorious figures at the Cais Dourado (Golden Wharf) in Salvador during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. His name appears among the legendary practitioners of the era, specifically noted for his expert handling of the navalha (straight razor)—a weapon that became synonymous with the street capoeira of that period.

Caboclinho's life centered on the waterfront. As a saveirista, he was part of the maritime economy that connected Salvador to the Recôncavo Baiano, transporting goods like cachaça, tobacco, and sugar from ports like Santo Amaro and Cachoeira. The Cais Dourado was his stronghold—a universe of dock workers, stevedores, porters, and sailors who practiced capoeira amid their labor. The waterfront district of Pilar and the surrounding markets were notorious gathering points where capoeiristas congregated, played, and inevitably clashed.

He was "always a target of the police headed by José Alvaro Cova, a man who entered history as the greatest persecutor of capoeiras." Yet Caboclinho also had access to Major Cosme de Farias—the famous rábula (self-taught lawyer) who became known as "the greatest defender of capoeiras," intervening in legal proceedings and serving as "the lawyer of the disadvantaged." This duality was typical of the era: capoeiristas existed in a liminal space between persecution and protection, often serving as political muscle (capangas) while simultaneously being hunted by the same authorities.

One documented incident captures Caboclinho's violent world. On the Tabuão—a steep street connecting the Upper City (Cidade Alta) to the Lower City (Cidade Baixa)—he fought with Mangue Sêco, another capoeirista of the docks. Caboclinho "delivered a violent razor blow" and was arrested afterward at the Cais Dourado. The Tabuão itself was infamous for capoeira violence, including the 1889 "Massacre do Tabuão" when capoeiristas of the Guarda Negra ambushed Republican demonstrators.

Caboclinho is listed among the "exímios manejadores da navalha" (expert razor handlers) of Bahia, alongside Inocêncio Sete Mortes and Noca de Jacó. This skill with the navalha was not merely criminal—it was a survival art in an era when the razor served as "the greatest symbol" of capoeiragem before the berimbau took its place. Practitioners wore silk scarves (esguião) around their necks specifically to deflect razor strikes.

His contemporaries at the Cais Dourado included the legendary Besouro Mangangá who arrived from Santo Amaro via saveiro, Pedro Porreta the fish seller who dominated the Baixa dos Sapateiros, Tico the porter, Antônio Corró the cart driver, and many others—Mata Escura, Manoel Thié, Marinheiro, Mangue Sêco himself, and figures like Ignácio Loyola, Pedro Mineiro, and Três Pedaços. Together they formed a community that kept capoeira alive through the years of criminalization (1890-1937).

Like many of his generation, Caboclinho's fate after the early 20th century is unknown. He remains one of the anonymous ancestors who preserved the art during its most dangerous years—an era when documentation came not from celebration but from police records and newspaper accounts of street violence.

Os praticantes usavam lenços de seda (esguião) no pescoço especificamente para desviar golpes de navalha.

Seus contemporâneos no Cais Dourado incluíam o lendário Besouro Mangangá que chegava de Santo Amaro via saveiro, Pedro Porreta o vendedor de peixe que dominava a Baixa dos Sapateiros, Tico o carregador, Antônio Corró o carroceiro, e muitos outros—Mata Escura, Manoel Thié, Marinheiro, o próprio Mangue Sêco, e figuras como Ignácio Loyola, Pedro Mineiro e Três Pedaços. Juntos formavam uma comunidade que manteve a capoeira viva durante os anos de criminalização (1890-1937).

Como muitos de sua geração, o destino de Caboclinho após o início do século XX é desconhecido. Ele permanece como um dos ancestrais anônimos que preservaram a arte durante seus anos mais perigosos—uma era quando a documentação não vinha da celebração, mas de registros policiais e relatos jornalísticos de violência de rua.

## Historical Context / Contexto Histórico

### Cais Dourado (Golden Wharf)

The Cais Dourado was a concentration point for capoeiristas in the late 19th century and first decades of the 20th century. Located in the Pilar district near the waterfront, it was a universe of workers—dock hands, porters, stevedores, saveiristas—who breathed the culture of sailors and practiced capoeira in their daily lives.

The area included the Praça do Ouro, various taverns (botequins), and the Mercado do Ouro—important commercial spaces. It witnessed frequent clashes between capoeiristas and police, while also serving as territory controlled by various capoeira figures.

Notable capoeiristas associated with Cais Dourado:
- Besouro de Mangangá (arrived from Santo Amaro via saveiro)
- **Caboclinho** (Alfredo Martins Teixeira, saveirista)
- Pedro Porreta (fish seller)
- Tico (Julio dos Santos Menezes, porter from Santo Amaro)
- Antônio Corró (cart driver)
- Mata Escura, Manoel Thié, Marinheiro, Mangue Sêco
- Ignácio Loyola, Pedro Piroca, Pedro Mineiro
- Antonio Boca de Porco, Gabiano, Agripino
- Paulo Gomes, José Martins, Cypriano, Três Pedaços

### The Navalha (Razor)

The navalha was the iconic weapon of late 19th/early 20th century capoeira, before the berimbau became the dominant symbol. Known by various names (naifa, nafe, sardinha), it was introduced to Brazilian capoeiristas by Portuguese fadistas in the 19th century.

Expert navalha handlers of Bahia included:
- **Caboclinho** (Alfredo Martins Teixeira)
- Inocêncio Sete Mortes
- Noca de Jacó

Rio de Janeiro had its own legendary razor masters: Manduca da Praia, Natividade, and Madame Satã.

Capoeiristas wore silk scarves (esguião) around their necks to protect against razor strikes—the silk would cause the blade to slide rather than cut.

### The Tabuão

The Tabuão was a steep street connecting the Cidade Alta (Upper City) to the Cidade Baixa (Lower City) in Salvador. It was infamous for capoeira violence:
- Site of Caboclinho's documented fight with Mangue Sêco
- Location of the 1889 "Massacre do Tabuão" when Macaco Beleza led the Guarda Negra against Republican demonstrators
- Pedro Mineiro was documented as living on the Tabuão
- Setting in Jorge Amado's "A Tenda dos Milagres"

### Major Cosme de Farias (1875-1972)

Cosme de Farias was a rábula (self-taught lawyer) known as "o advogado dos pobres" (the lawyer of the poor). He defended over 30,000 clients including many capoeiristas, often using insanity defenses to free them from charges.

Key facts:
- Born April 2, 1875, in Paripe, Salvador
- Only primary school education
- Major in the Guarda Nacional (rank received 1909)
- Founded Liga Baiana contra o Analfabetismo (1915)
- Died March 14, 1972
- A neighborhood in Salvador bears his name

Capoeiristas he defended included Pedro Porreta, Chico Três Pedaços, and many others who frequented the Cais Dourado.

### José Alvaro Cova

José Alvaro Cova was a police chief known as "o maior perseguidor dos capoeiras" (the greatest persecutor of capoeiras). However, historical sources reveal complexity: some capoeiristas like Pedro Mineiro were documented as his "capangas" (henchmen), protected by him while working as political enforcers. He was also connected to Governor José Joaquim Seabra (1855-1942).

This illustrates the ambivalent position of capoeiristas in the Old Republic (República Velha): simultaneously persecuted and protected, depending on political allegiances.
