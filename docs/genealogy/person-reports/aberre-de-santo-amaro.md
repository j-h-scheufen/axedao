## The "Two Aberrês" Controversy / A Controvérsia dos "Dois Aberrês"

### English

This profile documents a **possible second individual** named "Aberrê" who may have been distinct from the well-documented Antônio Raimundo Argolo (Aberrê de Salvador, 1895-1942).

**Key Evidence for Two Separate Persons:**

1. **Different Full Names:**
   - Aberrê de Salvador: Antônio **Raimundo** Argolo (birth records confirm)
   - Aberrê de Santo Amaro: Antônio **Rufino** dos Santos (per multiple sources about Caiçara)

2. **Caiçara's Own Testimony:**
   According to sources, Mestre Caiçara himself distinguished between the two masters:
   - His teacher: "José **Rufino**"
   - Pastinha's ex-student (who invited Pastinha to Gengibirra): "José **Raimundo**"

3. **Different Origins:**
   - Aberrê de Salvador: Born in Salvador
   - Aberrê de Santo Amaro: From Santo Amaro (Recôncavo Baiano)

4. **Student Denials:**
   Both Mestre Canjiquinha and Mestre Caiçara consistently denied connection to Pastinha's lineage, which is puzzling if they both trained under the same Aberrê who invited Pastinha to Gengibirra.

**Counter-Evidence (Single Person Theory):**

1. Both lineages reference an Aberrê who died and gave Caiçara his bengala
2. The death in September 1942 is only documented for the Salvador Aberrê
3. "Raimundo" and "Rufino" could theoretically be transcription errors, though they are phonetically distinct

## Biography / Biografia

### English

Little is known about this individual beyond his role as Mestre Caiçara's teacher. If he existed as a separate person from Aberrê de Salvador, he was likely active in the capoeira scene of the Recôncavo Baiano (the region around the Bay of All Saints) in the early 20th century.

**What We Know:**

In 1938, a 14-year-old Antônio Conceição Moraes (later Mestre Caiçara) began learning capoeira from "Mestre Aberrê de Santo Amaro." According to multiple sources, this teacher's full name was Antônio Rufino dos Santos. Aberrê taught Caiçara "the secrets and mysteries of capoeiragem" (the practice of capoeira).

The bengala (walking cane) that became Mestre Caiçara's trademark was given to him by his Mestre Aberrê before the mestre's death. According to Caiçara, his master also carried a bengala—both for practical use and as a sign of elegance. In the hands of a capoeirista, such a cane could become a deadly weapon.

**Timeline Estimation:**

If Aberrê de Santo Amaro was teaching in 1938, he was likely born sometime between the 1880s and early 1900s. A mestre teaching a new student would typically be at least in his 30s-40s, suggesting birth around 1890-1905.

**Death:**

The timing of his death is unclear. The documented Aberrê de Salvador died in September 1942. If there were two Aberrês:
- Aberrê de Santo Amaro may have died around the same time (explaining why both students' accounts reference a deceased teacher)
- Or he may have lived longer, with the bengala transfer happening at a different time

## Open Questions / Questões Abertas

1. **Are they the same person?** The name discrepancy (Raimundo vs Rufino) and origin difference (Salvador vs Santo Amaro) suggest two people, but this could be documentation error.

2. **Death date:** Did Aberrê de Santo Amaro also die around 1942, or did he live longer?

3. **Why did both students deny Pastinha lineage?** If Canjiquinha trained under Salvador's Aberrê (who invited Pastinha), why would he deny the connection?

4. **Did they know each other?** If two Aberrês existed in the same era, did they ever meet or play together?

5. **Santo Amaro capoeira scene:** What was the capoeira environment in Santo Amaro in the 1930s? Were there other mestres there?

## Academic Significance / Significância Acadêmica

As noted by researchers at [IPCB-RJ](http://ipcb-rj.com.br/?p=617):

> "Se a existência de dois Aberrês for verdade, a história da capoeira angola baiana precisará ser reescrita no futuro, pois seu legado é tão importante quanto de qualquer outro Mestre."

("If the existence of two Aberrês is true, the history of Bahian capoeira angola will need to be rewritten in the future, as his legacy is as important as any other Mestre.")

This question affects how we understand the lineages flowing from Aberrê through Canjiquinha and Caiçara—two of the most influential figures in spreading Capoeira Angola throughout Brazil.
