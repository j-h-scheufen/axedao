## Biography / Biografia

### English

Antônia de Tal, known by her apelido Cattú (also spelled Catu), was one of the legendary valentonas of early 20th century Salvador, Bahia. Her story survives through a remarkable newspaper account from February 10, 1914, published in the Jornal de Notícias, which documented an extraordinary confrontation with police forces.

The newspaper described her as "Mulher Perigosa" (Dangerous Woman), noting that Antônia, "muito conhecida por Cattú" (well known as Cattú), resided on Rua do Polytheama in Salvador - a street near the Teatro Politeama, one of the major cultural centers of the era. The account characterized her as "uma mulher de cabelinho na venta" - a colloquial expression meaning a woman not to be trifled with.

The 1914 incident began when Cattú was called to the police station and failed to appear. When civil guard #119 was sent to bring her in, she attacked him, ripping the buttons from his tunic and tearing his shirt to shreds. A fierce struggle ensued. The guard called for reinforcements, and an extraordinary response followed: guards numbered 64, 04, 14, and 203 arrived, along with a police regiment musician, two soldiers, and a sergeant.

What happened next became legendary: "a perigosa mulher lutou com todos" - the dangerous woman fought them all. Eight law enforcement personnel could not easily subdue one woman. She was only brought to the police station "com muita dificuldade" - with great difficulty. In the struggle, several guards were injured, including guard #119, who was bitten on the hand and had a finger hurt.

This documented incident exemplifies the skills and ferocity of the valentonas who inhabited Salvador's streets during the era of capoeira criminalization. The handling of her own body as a weapon - the tearing, biting, and sustained combat against multiple opponents - points to capoeira training. Such women were companions of the navalha (straight razor), canivete (pocket knife), and punhal (dagger), weapons that marked the old practitioners of capoeiragem after slavery.

Cattú stands alongside other legendary valentonas of Bahia: Rosa Palmeirão, Massú, Salomé, Chicão, Maria Doze Homens, Angélica Endiabrada, Almerinda, and Menininha. Their stories involved confrontations with police, razor fights, and even the deaths of famous male valentões. These women carved out their reputations through undeniable skill, refusing to accept the constraints society placed upon them.

Essas mulheres construíram suas reputações através de habilidade inegável, recusando-se a aceitar as restrições que a sociedade lhes impunha.

## The February 1914 Incident / O Incidente de Fevereiro de 1914

### English
The most detailed account of Catu's life comes from the Jornal de Notícias, February 10, 1914:

> "Mulher Perigosa, Antônia de tal muito conhecida por Cattú, residente na Rua do Polytheama, é uma mulher de cabelinho na venta."
>
> (Dangerous Woman, Antônia de tal, well known as Cattú, residing on Rua do Polytheama, is a woman not to be trifled with.)

Catu had been called to the police station multiple times and did not appear. When guard #119 was sent to arrest her, she attacked him, tearing off his tunic buttons and ripping his shirt to shreds. The guard called for help, and guards numbered 64, 04, 14, 203 responded, along with a police regiment musician, 2 soldiers, and 1 sergeant.

Despite facing eight law enforcement personnel, "a perigosa mulher lutou com todos" (the dangerous woman fought them all). She was only brought to the station "com muita dificuldade" (with great difficulty). Guard #119 was bitten on the hand and had a finger injured; several other guards were also hurt.

O relato mais detalhado da vida de Catu vem do Jornal de Notícias, 10 de fevereiro de 1914:

The Politeama was an important theater that presented plays, operas, and other spectacles. This location in the historic center was part of the urban landscape where capoeiristas and valentonas operated.
