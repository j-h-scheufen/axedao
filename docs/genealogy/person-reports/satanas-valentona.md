## Biography / Biografia

### English

Satanás was one of the few women who practiced capoeira in Salvador during the 1930s and 1940s - an era when the art was deeply hostile to female participation. She was part of a small but determined group of women who reportedly "se fizeram passar por homens" (disguised themselves as men) to gain acceptance in the male-dominated rodas of capoeira.

According to the capoeira oral tradition, she received her cruel apelido "Satanás" (Satan) because "por ser muito feia ninguém a queria" (being very ugly, no one wanted her). While such nicknames seem harsh by modern standards, they reflect the rough street culture of early 20th century Bahian capoeiragem, where apelidos often referenced physical characteristics, circumstances of life, or notorious deeds.

Satanás is listed alongside Maria 12 Homens, Calça Rala, Nega Didi, and Maria Pára o Bonde as one of the "grandes referências femininas" (great female references) of strength, courage, and determination from this era. These women became known as valentonas - brave, tough women who defied social norms to practice an art form that was both criminalized and explicitly coded as masculine.

Some sources indicate that Satanás was among the students of Mestre Bimba, who opened Salvador's first formal capoeira academy. Bimba was notable for accepting women into his academy during a time when this was controversial. However, he faced ongoing challenges: his female classes reportedly never completed full courses due to social pressures including interference from jealous boyfriends, pregnancies, and romantic entanglements with male students.

Sources note that Satanás "viveu sem registros" (lived without records), meaning no civil name, specific dates, or detailed biographical information survive. Like many women of her era, her story exists primarily in the realm of oral tradition and collective memory, preserved in the mentions by later researchers documenting the forgotten history of women in early capoeira.

## Origin of Apelido / Origem do Apelido

The nickname "Satanás" (Satan) reportedly originated from her physical appearance. According to the Espeto Capoeira source:

> "Por ser muito feia ninguém a queria, por isso recebeu esse maldoso apelido."
> (Being very ugly no one wanted her, so she received this cruel nickname.)

This reflects the harsh naming conventions of early capoeiragem, where physical characteristics - whether complimentary or cruel - often became permanent markers of identity.

## Documentation Gap / Lacuna de Documentação

Satanás is described as having "viveu sem registros" (lived without records). This means:
- No civil name recorded
- No specific dates beyond "1930s-1940s"
- No photographs or documents
- Story preserved only through oral tradition

This documentation gap is common for women in early capoeira, who faced:
1. Social exclusion from formal capoeira circles
2. Criminalization of capoeira until 1940
3. Patriarchal record-keeping that ignored women's contributions
4. Oral rather than written tradition
