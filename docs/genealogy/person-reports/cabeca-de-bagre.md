## Biography / Biografia

### English

Cabeça de Bagre is known only from a single historical reference: the Jornal Metropolitano (1960) listed him among the outstanding capoeiristas of the era in Salvador's Liberdade neighborhood, alongside Mestre Bimba, Mestre Waldemar, Mestre Traíra, and Espinho Remoso.

This mention places him among the most notable practitioners of his time. The Liberdade neighborhood was the center of Bahian capoeira in the 1950s—home to Mestre Waldemar's legendary Barracão where every Sunday capoeiristas gathered from across the city, and where Espinho Remoso held his own influential roda at Jaqueira do Carneiro. To be named alongside these figures suggests Cabeça de Bagre was a significant practitioner, though his specific contributions, lineage, and fate remain undocumented.

His apelido "Cabeça de Bagre" (Catfish Head) follows the common capoeira tradition of animal-based nicknames. The bagre (catfish) is a fish known for its disproportionately large head relative to its body, with a small brain. In Brazilian popular culture, the expression "cabeça de bagre" is used to describe someone foolish or slow-witted—in football, it refers to a player who misses easy goals. However, capoeira apelidos were frequently given ironically, to describe physical characteristics, or to commemorate a particular incident during training or in the roda. Without further documentation, the specific reason for this nickname remains unknown.

Despite extensive research across academic sources, historical archives, capoeira databases (including Velhos Mestres, CapoeiraHub, Lalaue, Papoeira, CapoeiraWiki), and web searches, no additional information about Cabeça de Bagre has been located. The Jornal Metropolitano article itself has not been digitized, and the specific date, author, and context of the mention are unknown—the reference comes from the Wikipedia article on Mestre Espinho Remoso.

Cabeça de Bagre represents one of the many capoeiristas whose contributions were essential to the art's survival but whose stories were not recorded in written history. In an oral tradition where practitioners deliberately hid their identities to avoid police persecution, many names were lost. That this apelido survived in a 1960 newspaper reference is itself remarkable—suggesting that Cabeça de Bagre's reputation in the rodas of Liberdade was significant enough to merit public recognition.
